# The Instagram Guide

**Good: this week — How the publication shows up on Instagram, what posts weekly, and how to generate new tiles without breaking the system.**

Version 1.0 — April 2026

---

## Part one: What Instagram is for

Instagram is not where Good: this week lives. It's where readers find the publication, decide whether the voice is for them, and click through to the website. Every decision in this guide flows from that.

That means the job of a post is not to go viral. It is to earn a three-second pause on a crowded feed, land one good headline, and send the reader to goodthisweek.news. A post that gets 10,000 likes but zero reads on the site is a failure. A post that gets 200 likes and sends 40 people to the homepage is a win.

This framing matters because it cuts a lot of options off the table. No Reels. No trending audio. No face-to-camera. No twelve-part explainer threads. The publication doesn't need to perform on Instagram. It just needs to advertise the newsletter clearly, in its own voice, in a format that rewards the scroll-stopper: text-on-color, designed tiles, and the occasional stat that punches.

> The best Instagram post for this publication is the one a reader screenshots and texts to a friend.
>
> — North star

## Part two: The posting rhythm

One carousel per week is the anchor. Everything else flexes around it.

### Sundays — the weekly carousel

The carousel publishes Sunday morning, the same day the newsletter goes out. It's a **6- to 8-slide permanent feed post** built from the week's issue. Not ten slides. Not four. Six to eight, depending on how many stories in the issue have genuinely strong visual hooks.

This is the one post that takes real effort. If nothing else ships that week, the carousel ships.

### Monday through Saturday — the daily story

One 24-hour **story post** per day, each pulling a single story from the week's issue. These disappear after 24 hours, which is a feature, not a bug. The stakes are low. The template is simple. If a story flops, it's gone by morning.

Six stories a week means the carousel's seven or so stories get roughly one story spotlight each. Pick the strongest six. The shorts can be skipped at the story level; they already live in the Sunday roundup tile.

### Mid-week bonus — optional permanent single

One or two times a week, pick the strongest slide from Sunday's carousel — the stat tile with the biggest number, or a pull quote that landed well — and repost it as a single permanent feed post. Tuesday and Thursday work. Friday works if the week has been quiet.

This is the highest-leverage move in the entire system. The tile already exists. The caption takes five minutes. It keeps the profile grid from looking dead between Sundays, and it lets a strong tile do a second round of work.

**Do not do this more than twice a week.** Three singles plus a carousel plus six stories is a feed that looks like it's trying too hard.

### The volume, summed up

- 1 Sunday carousel (permanent, 6–8 slides)
- 1–2 mid-week singles (permanent, reused from carousel)
- 6 daily stories (disappearing, one per day)

That is the entire posting rhythm. Nothing else.

## Part three: The slide system

The Sunday carousel is a sequence of tiles. Every tile is one of five types. Knowing which type each story gets is what keeps the carousel from becoming eight variations of the same flat headline card.

### The five tile types

**Headline tile.** A story headline in large Georgia italic on a category-colored background. The default. Used when the story's strongest feature is the headline itself.

**Stat tile.** A single large number on a category-colored background, with a small caption below explaining what it means. Used when the story contains a number that's genuinely striking on its own — a percentage, a count, a drop. "83%" is a stat tile. "Several hundred" is not.

**Pull quote tile.** A short quote in Georgia italic on the cream background, with the attribution in small caps below. Cream with a black border, exactly like the editor's-note tile in the original mockup. Used when the story contains a quote under twelve words that lands without setup.

**Shorts roundup tile.** Cream background, black border. Small caps header "Also this week:" and three one-line shorts stacked vertically, each with its own tiny category dot. Used once per carousel, near the end. Replaces the need to give the shorts section its own full tiles.

**CTA tile.** Black background, gold accents. The closing slide. It says, in some variation: "17 good things happened this week. Read them all at goodthisweek.news." The tally number changes weekly. The rest stays put.

### How to decide which tile type a story gets

Run this short check in order. Stop at the first yes.

1. Does the story have a number that works on its own, with no setup? → **Stat tile.**
2. Does the story have a quote under twelve words that lands without the surrounding article? → **Pull quote tile.**
3. Is the story one of the week's shorts? → **Goes in the shorts roundup tile** (not its own slide).
4. Default: → **Headline tile.**

This rule exists because the easiest default is to make everything a headline tile, and then the carousel becomes eight identical-feeling cards. The rule forces variety.

### The shape of a typical carousel

A good Sunday carousel has a structure like this:

- **Slide 1:** The lead story, as a headline tile. This is the hook. The single most scroll-stopping headline of the week, full bleed, big type.
- **Slides 2–5:** Four more stories in mixed tile types. At least one should be a stat tile. At least one should be a pull quote. The rest are headline tiles. Order them for color variety, not for story importance.
- **Slide 6:** The shorts roundup tile.
- **Slide 7:** The CTA tile.

Seven slides. Some weeks the lead story needs a second slide for a follow-up headline, or there's an eighth story worth its own tile. Then it's eight slides. That's the ceiling. Don't go to ten just because Instagram allows it.

### The color ordering rule

Beyond tile type variety, the carousel also has to feel good as a sequence of swipes. The rule is simple:

**No two adjacent slides share the same warm/cool temperature.**

Warm categories: Heartwarming (amber), Kindness (pink), Community (coral), Technology (soft pink).
Cool categories: Science (blue), Health (teal), Wildlife (purple), Good laws (green).
Neutral: the cream pull-quote tile and the black CTA tile — these can go next to anything.

This means if slide 1 is amber (warm), slide 2 should be blue or teal or purple or green (cool), or cream/black (neutral). Slide 3 swings back. And so on. The effect, when the reader swipes, is a rhythm of visual contrast rather than a muddy run of similar colors.

If the week's content doesn't naturally alternate, use a neutral slide (pull quote or cream roundup) to break a run of same-temperature tiles. That's what the neutrals are for.

### What the carousel is not designed to do

It is not designed to look good as a 3×3 profile grid. The carousel is a swipe-through sequence. Over time, as carousels and singles accumulate on the profile, the grid will naturally look balanced because every tile uses the same system. There is no need to plan a 3×3 composition in advance. Don't try.

## Part four: The tile specs

Every tile is 1080×1080 pixels. Cream background is `#faf9f5`. Black is `#1a1a1a`. Gold is `#FAC775`.

### Headline tile

- **Size:** 1080×1080.
- **Background:** The story's category color, full bleed.
- **Padding:** 80px on all four sides.
- **Category kicker (top left):** Sans-serif, 22px, letter-spacing 0.25em, uppercase, weight 600. Color is the category's ink color (see Part five).
- **Headline (bottom, anchored to the 80px bottom margin):** Georgia, bold (weight 900), italic, 64–84px depending on headline length. Line-height 1.05. Letter-spacing -0.01em. Color is the category's ink color.
- **Rule for sizing the headline:** fit it to two or three lines, never one, never four. If the headline is too long for three lines at 64px, rewrite it shorter. Don't shrink below 60px.

### Stat tile

- **Size:** 1080×1080.
- **Background:** The story's category color, full bleed.
- **Composition:** Centered vertically and horizontally. No kicker at top.
- **The number (top of the composition):** Georgia, bold (weight 900), not italic, 280–360px depending on how many characters. Line-height 0.9. Letter-spacing -0.03em. Color is cream `#faf9f5` on dark backgrounds, `#1a1a1a` on light backgrounds (see Part five).
- **The caption below the number:** Sans-serif, 32px, uppercase, letter-spacing 0.15em, weight 600, line-height 1.3. Two to three lines maximum. The caption's color is the category's soft ink (lighter variant) so it recedes behind the number.
- **Rule for the number:** percentage signs and currency symbols get the same size as the digits. No subscript. No "approximately."

### Pull quote tile

- **Size:** 1080×1080.
- **Background:** Cream `#faf9f5`.
- **Border:** 6px solid black `#1a1a1a`, inset from the tile edge by 30px. So a black frame with cream around it.
- **Padding inside the border:** 60px on all sides.
- **The quote:** Georgia, italic, 48–56px. Line-height 1.3. Color black `#1a1a1a`. Opens with a large gold pull-quote mark (Georgia italic 140px, `#FAC775`) floated to the upper-left before the first word.
- **Attribution (bottom):** Sans-serif, 20px, letter-spacing 0.2em, uppercase, weight 600, color `#5F5E5A`. Preceded by an em-dash (used as punctuation, not rhythm).

### Shorts roundup tile

- **Size:** 1080×1080.
- **Background:** Cream `#faf9f5`.
- **Border:** 6px solid black `#1a1a1a`, inset from the tile edge by 30px.
- **Padding inside the border:** 60px.
- **Header (top):** Georgia, italic, 44px, weight 900, color `#1a1a1a`. Reads "Also this week."
- **Three shorts stacked vertically:** Each short is Georgia, 28px, line-height 1.35, color `#1a1a1a`. Each short gets a small colored dot (14px, the category color) floated in the left margin, vertically aligned to the first line.
- **Spacing between shorts:** 40px.
- **Footer (small, bottom):** Sans-serif, 18px, uppercase, letter-spacing 0.2em, color `#5F5E5A`. Reads "More on the site."

### CTA tile

- **Size:** 1080×1080.
- **Background:** Black `#1a1a1a`, full bleed.
- **Composition:** Centered.
- **The masthead (top of composition):** Georgia, italic, 120px, weight 900, letter-spacing -0.04em, color cream `#faf9f5`. Reads "Good:". Line below, Georgia, italic, 60px, color `#FAC775` (gold). Reads "this week."
- **The tally line (below masthead, 40px gap):** Georgia, italic, 32px, color `#D3D1C7` (soft cream). Reads "[N] good things happened this week."
- **The CTA pill (bottom, anchored 80px from bottom):** Pill-shaped button rendered into the tile (not interactive, it's an image). Background gold `#FAC775`. Text inside, sans-serif, 28px, uppercase, letter-spacing 0.1em, weight 700, color `#1a1a1a`. Reads "Read at goodthisweek.news."

### A quick note on rendering

These tiles get rendered as HTML or SVG in a chat, then screenshotted at 1080×1080 for posting. Claude should generate them as self-contained HTML blocks that render correctly when screenshot. The specs above are the output target. If something doesn't look right at 1080×1080 when screenshot, the specs are wrong, not the rendering — report back and we'll fix the guide.

## Part five: The color system

The eight category colors come straight from the website. Do not invent new ones. Do not tweak the hex values for Instagram. Consistency across the site and the feed is the single most important piece of visual identity the publication has.

### The eight categories and their colors

| Category | Background | Ink (primary text) | Soft ink (captions) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heartwarming | `#FAC775` (amber) | `#1a1a1a` (black) | `#412402` (deep brown) |
| Science | `#378ADD` (blue) | `#faf9f5` (cream) | `#B5D4F4` (pale blue) |
| Health | `#1D9E75` (teal) | `#faf9f5` (cream) | `#9FE1CB` (pale teal) |
| Kindness | `#D4537E` (pink) | `#faf9f5` (cream) | `#FBEAF0` (pale pink) |
| Wildlife | `#7F77DD` (purple) | `#faf9f5` (cream) | `#CECBF6` (pale purple) |
| Community | `#D85A30` (coral) | `#faf9f5` (cream) | `#F5C4B3` (pale coral) |
| Technology | `#F4C0D1` (soft pink) | `#1a1a1a` (black) | `#5F5E5A` (charcoal) |
| Good laws | `#C0DD97` (green) | `#173404` (deep green) | `#27500A` (olive) |

### The ink rule

On dark backgrounds (Science, Health, Kindness, Wildlife, Community) the text is cream `#faf9f5`. On light backgrounds (Heartwarming, Technology, Good laws) the text is either black `#1a1a1a` or a deep version of the background color (deep brown on amber, deep green on green).

Never put cream text on a light background. Never put black text on a dark background. The soft ink column is only for small metadata-type text like stat captions — use it sparingly and never for headlines.

### The neutral tiles

The pull quote tile and shorts roundup tile use cream `#faf9f5` with a black border. This is deliberate. The neutrals are what keep the carousel from becoming a Skittles commercial. Every carousel needs at least one neutral slide somewhere in the middle of the sequence.

### The black-and-gold tile

The CTA tile is the only place gold `#FAC775` shows up as the primary accent color. Gold is not a general-purpose brand color. It's the masthead color, used only on the closing slide and in the avatar. Don't scatter gold accents across other tiles. The discipline is what makes the masthead feel like a masthead.

## Part six: Typography

Two fonts. That's it.

### Georgia

Used for all display type: headlines, pull quotes, the masthead, stat numbers. Almost always italic for headlines and quotes. Upright (roman) for stat numbers — italic digits read as unstable. Weight 900 for headlines and masthead, weight 400 for pull quote body text.

Georgia is the publication's voice in visual form. It's the same typeface used on the website. The consistency is the point.

### System sans-serif (`-apple-system, sans-serif`)

Used only for small utility text: category kickers, stat captions, CTA button text, attribution lines, "Also this week" footers. Never for headlines. Never for quotes.

The rule is: **if the text is content, it's Georgia. If the text is a label, it's sans.** A headline is content. A kicker that says "WILDLIFE" is a label. A quote is content. An attribution is a label.

### Letter-spacing

- **All uppercase sans text** gets `0.2em` to `0.3em` of letter-spacing. Uppercase without letter-spacing looks crushed.
- **Georgia headlines** get slightly negative letter-spacing (`-0.01em` to `-0.03em`) because Georgia italic has generous side-bearings that can look loose at large sizes.
- **Georgia body-size italic** (pull quotes) gets no letter-spacing adjustment.

### Font size at the tile scale

Numbers in the specs above assume a 1080×1080 render. When you screenshot that at the actual size Instagram serves (scaled to fit a phone), the type will be smaller but the proportions will hold. Always design and screenshot at 1080×1080. Never try to design at phone scale — you'll overcompensate and make the type ugly on desktop previews.

## Part seven: The story posts

The Monday-through-Saturday story posts are simpler than the carousel. They disappear after 24 hours, which means the design effort should be proportional — lightweight, fast to generate, consistent across the week.

### The daily story template

- **Size:** 1080×1920 pixels (9:16 vertical).
- **Background:** The story's category color, full bleed.
- **Padding:** 80px horizontal, 120px top, 180px bottom (leaves room for the CTA at the bottom).
- **Progress bar (top, 40px from the top edge):** A short horizontal rule, full width of the padded area, 4px thick. Cream `#faf9f5` on dark backgrounds, black `#1a1a1a` on light backgrounds.
- **Avatar row (below progress bar, 40px gap):** 48px circular avatar (black circle, gold Georgia italic "G:" inside), then "good.thisweek" in sans 22px, weight 600, then a light gray timestamp in sans 18px. Same ink color logic as the category.
- **Series tag (below avatar row, 40px gap):** A small solid pill. Black background, gold text. Sans 18px, uppercase, letter-spacing 0.25em, weight 600. Reads "Story 1 of 6." The number changes each day.
- **Category kicker (below series tag, 40px gap):** Sans 22px, uppercase, letter-spacing 0.2em, weight 600, color is the soft ink variant of the category. Prefixed with a small star glyph or bullet.
- **The headline (below kicker, 40px gap):** Georgia, italic, weight 900, 72–96px. Line-height 1.05. Color is the category's ink color. Three to four lines maximum.
- **The pull quote or supporting line (below headline, 40px gap, optional):** Georgia, italic, 32px, color is the category's soft ink. Used when the story has a strong quote — otherwise skip it.
- **The tap prompt (bottom, anchored to 180px from the bottom):** Sans 22px, uppercase, letter-spacing 0.2em, weight 700, color is the category's ink color. Reads "Tap for more on goodthisweek.news" or "Swipe up" or "Link in bio." Above it, a hairline rule (1.5px, same color).

### The "1 of 6" counter

The series tag is the one piece of the story post that creates a reason to come back the next day. Even if the reader only sees Monday's story, the "1 of 6" tells them there are five more coming. It is the lowest-effort engagement mechanic available and it costs you nothing to include.

Rule: always show the count. Even on Tuesday when nobody watched Monday's story. The count is for the reader's sense of the week, not for retention metrics.

### What not to do in stories

- No polls, no question stickers, no "swipe up" sliders in v1. They add interaction but they also add effort and inconsistency. Keep the stories design-consistent with the feed for the first three months. Add interaction later if and only if there's a reason.
- No GIFs layered on top. The tile is the content.
- No music. Text posts don't need audio.
- No reshares of other people's posts to your story. The publication has a voice. Borrowing someone else's feed for daily content dilutes it.

### Announcing a new issue

Sunday gets a special story post in addition to the feed carousel. It's the black masthead tile, full bleed, 9:16. It's the one from the original mockup. The copy is:

- **Kicker (top):** ISSUE NO. [N] IS OUT
- **Masthead:** Good: / this week
- **Supporting line (below masthead):** "[N] good things happened. Here's what we found."
- **CTA (bottom):** "↑ Read the issue"

This post goes up Sunday morning, right after the feed carousel. It's the only story that uses the black masthead background. Monday through Saturday's daily stories use category colors instead.

## Part eight: The captions

Captions have a different job from the tiles. The tile's job is to stop the scroll. The caption's job is to hold the reader long enough that they click the link in bio, or — at worst — remember the publication tomorrow. That means the first line of every caption has to earn the tap on "more."

Instagram cuts captions off at roughly 125 characters before the "... more" link. Everything that matters happens before that cutoff. Write the caption as if the "... more" is a cliff: what's above it is a real sentence; what's below it is context the curious reader pulls themselves through.

### The first-line rules

- Lead with a specific number, a named place, a dry observation, or a short quote fragment. Never a generic warm-up sentence. Never "Happy Sunday!" Never "This week's issue is out!"
- First line should be able to stand alone as its own small post. If you cut the rest, is the first line still something worth reading? If not, rewrite it.
- No emojis in the first line. One small emoji later in the caption is fine if it's earning its place. Usually: no emojis.

### The caption voice

Same voice as the newsletter. Plainspoken, dry, warm, specific. The style guide's rules on banned AI tells apply to captions as well as articles. No "it's not just X, it's Y" in captions either. No "palpable." No "journey" as a verb. A caption that breaks the voice breaks the voice — there's no separate "social media" register for this publication.

The one adjustment for captions versus articles: captions are allowed to be a little more direct about the CTA. The article ends one beat early. The caption, after doing its work, tells the reader where to go.

### The caption shape

Every Sunday carousel caption follows roughly this shape:

1. **The hook line** (above the "... more" fold). One sentence, specific, standalone. Usually pulls from the strongest story of the week. Example: "England is trying to bring back golden eagles for the first time in 150 years."
2. **The setup** (one or two sentences). A small amount of context that makes the hook land harder. Not a summary of the issue.
3. **The tease** (one or two sentences). A line or two hinting at the other stories in the issue, without listing them all. Example: "Also in this week's issue: a coffee study that is going to annoy some people, and a village in Portugal that adopted every stray dog."
4. **The CTA.** One line. "Full issue at goodthisweek.news — link in bio." That's it.
5. **Hashtags** (see below), on a separate line break, spaced down.

Total length: 400 to 700 characters. Do not write long captions. The post does not need to re-run the newsletter.

### Hashtags

Hashtags on Instagram do less than they used to, but they're not worthless. Use between three and six per post, no more. Mix one or two broad ("goodnews"), one or two niche ("positivenews," "goodnewsonly"), and one branded ("goodthisweek"). Put them at the end of the caption, on their own line.

Do not stuff twenty hashtags. Do not hide them in a comment. Do not use "#Sunday #feelgood #vibes" — those are signals to the algorithm that this is a generic inspirational post, which is exactly what Good: this week is trying not to be.

### Sample captions (reusable templates)

Four caption shells you can fill in. Replace the bracketed parts with the week's content.

**Template A — for a numbers-heavy week:**

```
[Most striking number of the week, as a full sentence with context].

[One sentence of additional color on that story].

Also in this week's issue: [one short phrase teasing another story], and [one short phrase teasing a third].

Full issue at goodthisweek.news — link in bio.

#goodnews #positivenews #goodthisweek
```

**Template B — for a lead-story week:**

```
[Lead story headline, rewritten as a natural sentence].

[One or two sentences on why this one matters, in the house voice].

Plus: [one-line tease of a second story], [one-line tease of a third].

Read the full issue at goodthisweek.news. Link in bio.

#goodnews #goodnewsonly #goodthisweek
```

**Template C — for a quote-led week:**

```
"[Short quote from one of the week's stories, under 15 words.]" — [attribution].

[One sentence of context for the quote].

Seven more good things in this week's issue, including [brief tease] and [brief tease].

goodthisweek.news. Link in bio.

#goodnews #positivenews #goodthisweek
```

**Template D — the daily story caption (for the 9:16 stories):**

Stories don't get long captions. They get a one-line tag below the visual, if anything. Example: "Full story in this week's issue. Link in bio."

### Never

- Never ask a question in the first line to "drive engagement." It's the oldest trick in the social-media-manager book and it reads as thirsty.
- Never use the phrase "Swipe through to see."
- Never tag publications or journalists whose work you're summarizing in a way that looks like you're asking for a reshare. If you're genuinely crediting, do it in the caption text, not as an @mention.
- Never start a caption with "Happy Sunday" or any weekday greeting.
- Never use "the team" or "we" in a way that implies a staff larger than one person. The publication is plainspoken about its scale.

## Part nine: The weekly workflow

The goal is Sunday-heavy, weekday-light. Roughly three hours of focused work on Sunday, then fifteen to twenty minutes per weekday to post that day's story.

### Sunday morning (90 minutes, working session)

1. Open the current issue JSON file (once the publishing workflow from the launch plan is built).
2. Start a new chat and paste the Instagram generation prompt (see Part ten).
3. Claude produces a carousel plan: which stories get which tile types, the slide order, and a copy-ready draft of each tile's text.
4. Review the plan. Push back on anything that doesn't feel right. Swap tile types. Reorder for color rhythm.
5. Claude generates the HTML/SVG for each tile at 1080×1080.
6. Screenshot each tile. Save to a folder named `issue-[N]-instagram`.

### Sunday, posting (20 minutes)

1. Open Instagram on the phone. Create new post.
2. Select all seven (or however many) carousel images in order.
3. Paste caption (generated alongside the tiles in step 3 above).
4. Review on phone one more time. Publish.
5. Immediately after: post the 9:16 masthead "new issue" story. Same session.

### Monday through Saturday (10 minutes each day)

1. Open the folder of pre-generated daily stories (generated in Sunday's session).
2. Post the day's story. That's it.

### The trick: generate all six daily stories on Sunday

Do not wait until Monday to design Monday's story. Do all six on Sunday, in the same session as the carousel. That way the weekday work is literally just "open Instagram, upload one image, post." If you try to generate them day-by-day you will skip a day in week four. Batch on Sunday.

### The mid-week single

Once on Tuesday or Thursday, repost one tile from Sunday's carousel as a permanent feed post. Use Template C from the captions section, or a shorter variant. Five minutes, no design work.

## Part ten: How to brief Claude for a weekly Instagram run

When you start a new chat to generate a week's Instagram content, paste this prompt. It assumes Claude has access to the issue's JSON data or the issue content pasted inline.

### The weekly generation prompt

```
I need to generate Instagram content for this week's issue of Good: this week. Follow docs/instagram-guide.md exactly — do not invent new tile types, colors, or formats.

Here is this week's issue content:

[PASTE ISSUE JSON OR STORY LIST WITH HEADLINES, KEY NUMBERS, QUOTES, CATEGORIES]

Produce the following, in this order:

1. A CAROUSEL PLAN (before generating any visuals). For each of 6–8 slides: which story, which tile type, which category color, and a one-line reason. Apply the color ordering rule (no two adjacent slides share warm/cool temperature). The lead slide is slide 1. The shorts roundup is second-to-last. The CTA is last.

2. The CAROUSEL CAPTION using Template A, B, or C from Part eight, whichever fits the week best. Under 700 characters. First line must work above the "... more" cutoff.

3. All CAROUSEL TILES rendered as individual HTML blocks, each self-contained, each sized to 1080×1080. Use exact hex colors and typography from Part four and Part five.

4. Six DAILY STORY TILES (1080×1920) — one per story I'll post Monday through Saturday. Use the daily story template from Part seven. Each gets its own "Story N of 6" tag.

5. The SUNDAY MASTHEAD STORY (1080×1920 black masthead, 9:16).

6. A ONE-PARAGRAPH SUMMARY of the week's set so I can see it at a glance before I start screenshotting.

Voice rules from docs/style-guide.md apply to all tile copy and captions. No "it's not just X, it's Y." No em-dashes as rhythm. No "palpable." No thesaurus words. End one beat early.

Push back if any of the week's stories don't have a strong enough hook for Instagram. I'd rather post 5 great tiles than 8 okay ones.
```

### The revision prompt

When the first draft misses, paste this alongside what needs to change.

```
Revise the Instagram tiles for this week. Specific issues:

[LIST WHAT'S WRONG — e.g., "slide 3 and slide 4 are both warm colors, break them up," "the stat tile number is too long, pick a different number from the story," "the CTA caption feels generic, make it specific to this week"]

Keep everything else. Don't rewrite tiles that weren't flagged.
```

### A note on working style

The Instagram guide is a system, not a set of templates to copy blindly. If a week's issue doesn't have a good stat, don't force a stat tile. If a week's lead story isn't visually strong, put the second story in slide 1 instead. The rules in this guide are the floor, not the ceiling. The ceiling is: does this week's set of posts look and sound like Good: this week? If yes, ship it.

## Part eleven: The pre-post checklist

Run this every Sunday before publishing. Three minutes. Catches most of what you'd otherwise regret Tuesday.

### The carousel

- Is slide 1 a genuine scroll-stopper, not a masthead or "Issue N" framing?
- Does the carousel have a mix of tile types, not all headline tiles?
- Does the color sequence alternate warm and cool, with neutrals as breaks?
- Is the final slide a CTA, not another story?
- Is every category color correct to the hex values in Part five?
- Every headline: would a stranger stop on it, with no context? If not, rewrite or skip.

### The caption

- Is the first line a real sentence that works above the "... more" fold?
- No banned AI tells? No "palpable," no "it's not just X"?
- Is the CTA exactly one line, linking to goodthisweek.news?
- Under 700 characters total?
- Three to six hashtags on their own line?

### The daily stories

- All six generated in advance and saved to the folder?
- Each has the correct "Story N of 6" counter?
- Each uses the story's actual category color, not a reused color from last week?
